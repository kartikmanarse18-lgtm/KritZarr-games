FACES IN THE WAKE

An interactive maritime investigation into one of the most controversial paranormal photographs ever taken. A story involving industrial death, ocean burial rituals, mass psychological trauma, forensic photography, and a ghostly image that has haunted maritime folklore for over a century.

I. THE SHIP

In December of 1924, the steam tanker known as the S.S. Watertown crossed the Pacific Ocean carrying crude petroleum toward the Panama Canal. The vessel belonged to the Cities Service Company, a major industrial shipping operation during the rise of American petroleum expansion.

Life aboard an oil tanker in the 1920s was brutally dangerous. Crew members worked in enclosed steel compartments surrounded by toxic gases, heat, flammable vapors, and primitive ventilation systems. One small error inside a confined compartment could become fatal within seconds.

VESSEL TYPE

Steam-powered oil tanker.

PRIMARY ROUTE

San Pedro → Pacific Coast → Panama Canal → Gulf Transit.

KNOWN INCIDENT

Two crewmen reportedly died while cleaning a cargo area contaminated with toxic petroleum gas.

II. THE DEATHS INSIDE THE COFFERDAM

The most chilling phase of the Watertown legend begins deep inside a narrow industrial compartment below deck. Crewmen James Courtney and Michael Meehan were reportedly assigned to clean the ship's cofferdam — a cramped steel cavity contaminated by petroleum residue and vapor.

Historical retellings claim that a concentrated pocket of toxic fumes overwhelmed both men before they could climb back to safety.

"When they were discovered, both men had collapsed inside the compartment before they could reach the ladder."

LIKELY CAUSE

Petroleum vapor inhalation combined with oxygen displacement in an enclosed space.

1920s SAFETY CONDITIONS

No modern gas sensors, minimal ventilation systems, and almost nonexistent confined-space safety regulations.

PSYCHOLOGICAL EFFECT

The deaths reportedly deeply traumatized the crew before the alleged sightings even began.

III. BURIAL AT SEA

Following maritime tradition, both men were reportedly sewn into weighted canvas shadows and committed to the Pacific Ocean.

According to later accounts, the sea was calm during the burial ceremony. Crew members gathered silently while prayers were read over the stern.

"Less than twenty-four hours later, some aboard the vessel claimed the dead had returned."

IV. THE FIRST SIGHTINGS

As sunset fell across the Pacific Ocean, the Watertown's stern wake began glowing beneath fading orange light. According to later retellings from crew members, the sea behind the vessel appeared strangely uniform — as though sections of foam were holding shape longer than normal.

At first, sailors dismissed the anomaly as exhaustion and stress following the deaths. But over the following evenings, more crew members reportedly gathered near the stern to observe the phenomenon.

"Some swore the faces were rising upward through the foam itself. Others claimed the eyes appeared open whenever moonlight struck the wake."

ENVIRONMENTAL CONDITIONS

Calm Pacific waters combined with long wake trails may have amplified recurring wave formations.

CREW CONDITION

Extreme fatigue, grief, oil vapor exposure, and isolation likely affected perception aboard the ship.

REPORTED EFFECT

Witnesses claimed the shapes remained visible for several evenings before fading completely near port.

Crew members reportedly began noticing unusual patterns forming inside the ship's wake. At first the shapes appeared to be random foam structures illuminated by sunset light.

But according to witness stories repeated over decades, the shapes slowly became recognizable as human faces.

"The faces were said to move with the wake itself, never falling behind the ship."

DAY 1

Burial at sea conducted.

DAY 2

Initial wake anomalies reportedly observed by lookout crew.

DAY 3

Rumors spread through the vessel. Some crew allegedly refused stern watch duty after dark.

DAY 4

Captain Keith Tracy reportedly attempts photographic documentation.

V. THE PHOTOGRAPH

By the fourth evening, Captain Keith Tracy reportedly decided to document the anomaly. Using a small onboard camera, he allegedly photographed the wake while multiple crew members stood nearby.

Most accounts claim several exposures failed completely before one final image captured what appeared to be two distorted human faces floating within the water.

"The image would later become one of the most famous ghost photographs ever published."

IMAGE CHARACTERISTICS

Dark orbital cavities, distorted facial contours, and unusually symmetrical foam structures dominate the composition.

PHOTOGRAPHIC LIMITATIONS

1920s maritime photography often suffered from blur, grain amplification, water reflection distortion, and exposure instability.

PUBLIC REACTION

The image rapidly entered paranormal magazines, maritime folklore archives, and ghost-photography collections.

The most famous element of the Watertown case is the photograph allegedly taken by Captain Keith Tracy. The image appears to show two distorted human faces emerging from ocean foam behind the vessel.

The image became one of the most reproduced paranormal photographs in modern folklore. Supporters argue the photograph captured an unexplained phenomenon. Skeptics argue the image demonstrates classic visual pareidolia.

PARANORMAL CLAIM

The faces resemble the deceased crewmen with unnatural clarity.

SKEPTICAL CLAIM

Random wave patterns combined with contrast enhancement created the illusion of faces.

UNRESOLVED ISSUE

The original negative was reportedly lost decades later, preventing modern forensic analysis.

VI. FORENSIC ANALYSIS LAB

Modern analysts have attempted to digitally reconstruct the famous image. Some claim the uncropped version reveals repeating ocean patterns that weaken the supernatural interpretation. Others argue the facial structures remain unusually recognizable.

PARANORMAL INTERPRETATION

Witness consistency, emotional reactions among crew members, and the unusual visibility of facial structures are frequently cited as evidence that something extraordinary occurred aboard the Watertown.

FORENSIC SKEPTICISM

Photographic experts point to selective cropping, high-contrast development methods, and the human tendency to recognize faces in random visual information.

VII. THE BURNS DETECTIVE AGENCY

As rumors surrounding the Watertown spread through maritime circles, company officials reportedly feared panic among commercial crews. Paranormal stories aboard industrial vessels threatened discipline, morale, and operational stability.

According to later accounts, the Burns Detective Agency examined the photograph in an effort to determine whether the image had been manipulated.

"Investigators reportedly searched for evidence of double exposure, retouching, or chemical tampering — but no definitive proof of forgery was ever publicly documented."

FORENSIC THEORY

Investigators allegedly examine the camera, negative development process, and supply chain for signs of manipulation.

MEDIA ESCALATION

The case increasingly shifted from maritime tragedy into paranormal legend.

ARCHIVE PROBLEM

Most original records associated with the case are either missing, incomplete, or impossible to verify today.

According to popular retellings of the case, the Cities Service Company allegedly requested an investigation into the authenticity of the photograph. The Burns Detective Agency was reportedly involved in examining the image and negative.

Some paranormal accounts claim investigators failed to identify signs of tampering. However, documentation surrounding the investigation remains incomplete and controversial.

"The line between folklore and documented history becomes increasingly blurred as the story moves through decades of retelling."

VIII. MODERN PSYCHOLOGY

Neuroscientists describe the phenomenon known as pareidolia as the brain's tendency to recognize meaningful patterns — especially human faces — within random visual noise.

This effect becomes dramatically stronger during periods of emotional stress, fear, fatigue, grief, or environmental isolation.

PAREIDOLIA

Humans instinctively search for faces in clouds, shadows, waves, and static.

GROUP SUGGESTION

Once one person identifies a face, others may begin perceiving the same structure.

MARITIME ISOLATION

Extended ocean voyages historically intensified superstition and psychological strain among crews.

IX. THE LOST NEGATIVE

One of the greatest mysteries surrounding the case is the disappearance of the alleged original photographic negative.

Without the source material, modern investigators cannot perform complete high-resolution forensic scanning, chemical emulsion analysis, or modern spectral reconstruction.

"Whether accidental, administrative, or deliberate, the disappearance of the original negative ensured the mystery would survive indefinitely."

X. FINAL ASSESSMENT

The S.S. Watertown remains one of the most famous paranormal maritime legends ever recorded.

To believers, the image represents direct evidence that the dead briefly returned to the surface of the Pacific Ocean. To skeptics, it demonstrates the extraordinary power of human perception, grief, suggestion, and visual interpretation.

Nearly a century later, the question remains unresolved.

Were the faces truly following the ship? Or did a traumatized crew see humanity reflected back at them from the sea itself?
