North London · August 1977

The Enfield Poltergeist

The voice that should not exist. The case that cannot be closed.

At 284 Green Street, Enfield, something took up residence that summer. Police officers saw things they could not explain. Journalists fled. Investigators spent a year inside those walls and came out forever changed. And from the mouth of an eleven-year-old girl came a voice — deep, ragged, ancient — that called itself Bill.

Chapter I · August 1977
01

The Night It Began

The First Movement

It was a warm August night when Peggy Hodgson heard her daughters shouting. She assumed they were playing — children always found excuses to stay up late. She pushed open their bedroom door, ready to scold them back to sleep.

The heavy pine chest of drawers was sliding across the floor. Slow. Deliberate. Moving toward the door. Nobody was touching it.

Peggy pressed it back against the wall. It moved again.

I pushed it back. It moved again. I knew then that something was very wrong in this house.

— Peggy Hodgson, mother of four

The Knocking in the Walls

What followed that first night was a sound Peggy would never forget: a rapid, rhythmic knocking from inside the walls — not pipes, not settling wood. Purposeful. When investigators later asked it to knock three times, it knocked three times. When they asked for four, it gave four.

The knocks came every night. Then they weren't enough. Something in 284 Green Street wanted more than walls. It wanted a voice.

Chapter II · September–October 1977
02

The House Becomes Violent

⚠ The following events were witnessed by police officers, journalists, and SPR investigators

Objects With Intent

There is a difference between chaos and malice. What happened inside 284 Green Street felt like malice. Objects did not merely fall — they were thrown. Marbles sailed across rooms and struck investigators on the back of the head. LEGO bricks whipped through the air in the dark. A heavy sofa flipped onto its side with a crash that brought the neighbors running.

Daily Mirror photographer Graham Morris was hit on the forehead by a Lego brick while taking photographs. He checked the room. The children were not near him.

  • LEGO bricks thrown across rooms with force, striking visitors
  • Curtains wrapping themselves around Janet's neck in the night
  • Furniture overturning with no contact observed
  • Matches igniting spontaneously on window ledges
  • Water pooling on floors from no visible source
  • Beds shaking so violently children were thrown from them
  • A heavy sofa lifted and overturned onto its face
  • Marbles striking investigators from behind in empty rooms

I saw the chair move. I walked over and examined it. I could find no reason for it to have moved.

— PC Carolyn Heeps, Metropolitan Police
Chapter III · November 1977 – June 1978
03

The Voice in the Walls

Bill

The knocking had stopped being enough. On a November night, Janet Hodgson — eleven years old, slight, terrified — opened her mouth. And from somewhere deep inside her came a voice that did not belong to an eleven-year-old girl.

It was low. Gravelled. Wet at the edges, like someone breathing through fluid. It spoke slowly, deliberately, in a way that made everyone in the room go very still.

It said its name was Bill Wilkins. That it had lived in this house. That before it died, it had gone blind. That it died in the chair downstairs. That it did not know why it was still here.

Before I died, I went blind, and then I had a haemorrhage and I fell asleep and I died in the chair in the corner downstairs.

— "Bill Wilkins," speaking through Janet Hodgson — recorded, 1977

The Recordings

Investigators Maurice Grosse and Guy Lyon Playfair recorded hours of the voice. What they captured defied simple explanation. Janet's vocal cords were examined by specialists — the rasping, deep voice was produced by her false vocal fold, a technique used unconsciously only under extreme stress or in very rare cases.

She maintained the voice for hours. She was sometimes taped to a chair — still the voice came. She was in another room — still the sounds continued through the walls.

Later, a son of Bill Wilkins confirmed: his father had lived at 284 Green Street. He had gone blind in his final years. He had died of a haemorrhage — in a chair — in the downstairs room.

Chapter IV · The Photographs
04

The Levitation Evidence

Photographs That Shocked Britain

Graham Morris had been a press photographer for years. He had covered accidents, disasters, crime scenes. He was not a man easily frightened. The night he spent at 284 Green Street changed him.

His photographs showed Janet suspended mid-air above her bed — not at the peak of a jump, but at impossible angles, limbs in positions no jump produces. In one image her body is twisted sideways in a way that gymnasts train years to achieve.

Skeptics said she was jumping. Believers pointed to the angles. The debate continues to this day.

Chapter V · The Investigation
05

The Men Who Stayed

Maurice Grosse & Guy Lyon Playfair

Most people who entered 284 Green Street left quickly. Maurice Grosse and Guy Lyon Playfair stayed for over a year.

Grosse had recently lost his daughter in a motorbike accident. He was not a man looking for ghosts — he was a man who needed to believe that something persisted after death. Playfair was a seasoned investigator, skeptical by training. Together they formed an unlikely team.

They catalogued over 2,000 separate incidents. They recorded the voices, photographed the levitations, documented the objects in flight. They were also, twice, caught out — Janet was found bending spoons. One incident involved banging a broomstick on the ceiling.

Grosse's response to this was not to close the case. It was to note it — and continue. Because the other 1,998 incidents had no such explanation.

  • 2,000+ documented incidents over 14 months
  • Audio recordings of the "Bill" voice — hours of material
  • Photographic evidence of apparent levitation
  • Independent witness testimony from police, journalists, neighbors
  • Voice analysis by acoustic experts
  • Confirmation of Bill Wilkins' existence and death by his family
  • Janet twice caught faking — both incidents documented honestly by Grosse

Chapter VI · Believers vs. Skeptics
06

The Case That Will Not Close

The Case For

  • Multiple independent witnesses including police officers
  • "Bill's" details confirmed by his living son
  • Voice sustained for hours — physically demanding technique
  • Events continued even when children were restrained
  • Grosse documented the faking incidents honestly — which raises credibility
  • 2,000 incidents — too many to be sustained performance

The Case Against

  • Janet admitted faking some incidents
  • Levitation photos consistent with jumping from bed
  • Grosse was emotionally invested after his daughter's death
  • Bill Wilkins' details were obtainable — obituaries, neighbors
  • Children starved for attention after parents' divorce
  • No physical evidence survives — only recordings and photos

We faked it once or twice to see if the investigators would notice. They didn't. But most of it we didn't fake. Most of it was real. We were terrified.

— Janet Hodgson, interview 2012

The Voice Still Exists

The recordings are real. The tapes are archived. You can still hear Bill Wilkins speaking through the mouth of an eleven-year-old girl — decades after the house at Green Street fell quiet.

Janet Hodgson grew up. She married. She had children of her own. She moved back to Green Street once, in adulthood — just to see. The house felt normal, she said. But she didn't stay the night.
